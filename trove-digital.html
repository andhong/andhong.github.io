Trove 3D Customizer

| User Research | Prototyping | Usability Testing | A/B Testing |

Overview

Trove is a customizable jewelry platform where users are able to discover, customize, and purchase their own unique jewelry pieces. The in-browser customization tools allows anyone to create unique pieces regardless of their experience level with design programs.

My Role

I co-founded Trove alongside Brian Park and Tim Growney in 2015. In addition to designing the physical components of the product, I was the sole designer of the brand, site, and UX from top to bottom along with continual testing, refinement, and new feature development. Being the only designer for the platform helped me to maintain a consistent user experience from initial awareness of the Trove brand all the way to receiving and wearing our custom jewelry.
The Challenge

Custom Jewelry for All

The customized goods market grows yearly, especially with millenials who are looking for ways to express their individuality. In order to find unique jewelry, however, consumers usually work directly with a jewelry designer (which can be an extended and convoluted process). The purpose of the customization interface was to create a novel experience where any user would be able to customize their jewelry by making alterations to the shape, color, and size of jewelry and then purchase their unique items. This provided a series of challenges such as building an interface that was usable by people regardless of their exposure to design tools. In addition, the customizer needed to work seamlessly between web and mobile.
Research and Refine

Understanding the User

We first sought to understand our potential users - where they shop, the value they place on unique products, and whether or not they would customize jewelry for themselves or others. We soon discovered (and continued to discover with regular testing and customer feedback) that we expected two segments - users who wanted to express their own creativity, and users who wanted to find a unique gift. Both of these groups could then be segmented out with more specificity for ad retargeting and to inform product design choices. A third segment that we did not focus on was early adopters, primarily male, who were interested in the technology alone.
Design Process

Beginning with E-Commerce

In parallel with the development of the 3D Customizer, I also needed to design the e-commerce portion of the Trove website that the Customizer would then connect to. Because of file export requirements and some other functionality related to the Customizer, we were forced to build an e-commerce platform from scratch.

I gathered all of the user research and turned those learnings into the strategic direction of the website - both its e-commerce and customization components - using a range of tools, some of which are described below:

Concept Mapping

One of the major challenges was determining how the customizer would fit within a standard e-commerce site flow - what options would be made available at what points, and how to allow for people to buy customized products from others when they didn't want to make something themselves.

Paper Prototypes

My personal workflow involves a lot of sketching on paper for low fidelity prototypes to focus on the information architecture and flow of a design without getting distracted by aesthetics in Photoshop or other programs.

Mock Ups and Interactive Prototype Site

Because of development resource constraints (with our only engineer at the time working on the back end of the site), I quickly went from high fidelity mockups to building a clickable prototype site for the e-commerce component of the Trove platform.

Usability Testing and Reworking

I conducted numerous tests in order to track site navigation and ensure that users would be able to find the customization tools and that the tools fit into the users' mental models of how to move through a standard e-commerce site.
Designing the Customizer

(You the Real) MVP

Once the e-commerce portion of the site was at a workable place, I focused on designing the Customizer and making sure that it fit seamlessly with the rest of the Trove site.
After multiple rounds of wireframes, mockups, user tests, technical discussions, and refinements, I eventually landed on an interface for the customizer that presented users with a limited range of options and placed the jewelry front and center. The design also emphasized the collaborative element of the design process on Trove, where we had intended for users to be able to customize off of each others' designs to create a continual design narrative between modified objects.
Iterate, then Iterate some more

Starting Again

After several rounds of testing, we realized that the customization interface was still too complicated for older demographics. We sought to create a Customizer that provided a linear path through the numerous decisions that are possible with our custom jewelry. Each step would prompt users to continue on to a next step and provide incremental rewards to move users towards task completion.

Step by Step

I eventually landed on a Customizer that divides up options into individual steps. This was intended to break up the cognitive load of having multiple tasks before checkout and reduce perceived friction points in the customization process.
Reflections

Launching our first product

As the first major product launch for a new founding team, the Customizer proved to be an invaluable learning experience. Internally, we learned how to effectively communicate with one another and discuss ideal products versus feasibility given short timelines. We began the ongoing process of deeply understanding our users and continue to refine this knowledge in order to continue to build better products.

While it is drilled into UX designers to test, iterate, and refine, going through this process at a rapidly moving startup truly brought to light the urgency and importance of this process. Our success relies heavily on this design process and continual desire to explore and learn how we can innovate in ways that benefit our audience.
